# Fault Zone Theory

> A fault zone is a region between two negation channels where no mutual translation standard exists. This is not information asymmetry — it is **conceptual incommensurability**.

## Information Asymmetry vs. Fault Zone

- **Information asymmetry:** A knows something B doesn't. Solution: tell B what A knows.
- **Fault zone:** A and B use different conceptual systems to describe the same world. The "risk" A speaks of and the "risk" B speaks of are not the same thing. Solution: not transmitting information, but **manufacturing a translation standard** both sides can call upon.

A fault zone is the most invisible barrier to low-altitude economy industrialization. It is not an institutional barrier (policy can break those). It is not a technical bottleneck (R&D can overcome those). It is a **missing cognitive infrastructure** — two industries have each operated within their own conceptual systems for decades, and suddenly low-altitude economy pulls them to the same negotiating table, only to discover they cannot understand each other.

## Typology of Fault Zones

| ID | Fault Zone | Two Sides | Core Incommensurability |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| T1 | Military ↔ Civil Aviation | Military control logic ↔ Commercial operations logic | "Safety" defined incommensurably |
| T2 | Regulator ↔ Local Government | Industry regulation logic ↔ Local development logic | "Development" measured in different units |
| T3 | Government ↔ Operator | Public goods logic ↔ Profit logic | "Cost" aggregated differently |

| T5 | Operator ↔ Shipper | Capacity supply logic ↔ Logistics demand logic | Capacity units don't align with demand units |
| T6 | Insurer ↔ Operator | Actuarial logic ↔ Flight operations logic | Risk data format incommensurable |
| T7 | Equipment maker ↔ Operator | Technical standards logic ↔ Business model logic | Tech parameters don't map to business metrics |
| T8 | Investor ↔ Operator | Capital return logic ↔ Operational capacity logic | Return period narratives differ |

## The Translator

A translator does not relay messages between the two sides. Relaying does not solve conceptual incommensurability — translating A's words literally for B still leaves B unable to understand, because B's mind lacks A's conceptual framework.

A translator manufactures a **third negation channel**: a translation standard independent of both sides' conceptual systems. Each side describes the world in its own language. The translator maps both inputs to a common intermediate format, then outputs from that format to the other side.

**FaultLine Translator (FLT)** is the engineering implementation: terminology mapping + logic rules + data transformation. Not manual translation — engine-driven.

## Why Pricing Cannot Emerge Spontaneously from Markets

Mainstream economics holds that prices are "discovered" by supply and demand in markets. This presupposes that both sides use the same conceptual system — that what the buyer means by "I want this" and what the seller means by "I sell this" refer to the same thing.

In the low-altitude economy, the two sides of a fault zone cannot even agree on what "this" is. The "route risk" an operator speaks of and the "route risk" an insurer speaks of are two different things. A market cannot price something when participants cannot agree on what the thing *is*.

Pricing infrastructure must precede price discovery. The translator is the prerequisite to pricing infrastructure — without first filling the fault zone, the two sides can never reach the pricing step.
